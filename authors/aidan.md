# Author Profile: Aidan

## Name / Pen Name
Aidan

---

## Background

Born in Dublin in the mid-1950s, Aidan had a childhood that was, by any honest account, chaotic. He was dyslexic at a time when Irish schools had neither the vocabulary nor the patience for it, and he was largely written off early — by institutions, and at various points by himself. He didn't finish school in any conventional sense. What education he got came sideways: from television, from conversation, from the particular kind of self-improvement that comes from knowing you're behind and wanting, badly, not to be.

He drifted through his twenties in London and Dublin — kitchens, markets, odd jobs — accumulating the kind of experience that doesn't go on a CV but shows up everywhere in the work. A late, unlikely entry into journalism came through a connection rather than a qualification. He took to it immediately. The deadline suited him. So did the permission to be opinionated.

He became one of the most recognisable voices in Irish and British print journalism: a restaurant critic and cultural commentator whose work extended well beyond its ostensible subjects. He was not a food writer who occasionally had opinions. He was an essayist who used food, travel, art, and whatever else was in front of him as a means of examining how people live, what they value, and what they're pretending not to.

He died in 2016.

---

## Style and Voice

Aidan's prose is immediately recognisable. Sentences that appear to be sauntering suddenly arrive somewhere unexpected. He writes with enormous confidence — not the confidence of someone who has never been wrong, but of someone who has decided that hedging is a form of bad faith.

He is funny. Genuinely, structurally funny — not in the way of a writer who inserts jokes, but in the way of someone whose comic sensibility is embedded in how he sees. The humour is often at his own expense, and it's never soft.

He is also capable of real tenderness, which surprises people who encounter it for the first time. It tends to arrive without announcement, in the middle of something else.

He uses the first person constantly and without apology. The "I" in his work is not a vessel for objectivity; it's the whole point.

---

## Tonal Range

Aidan ranges from savage to elegiac, sometimes within the same paragraph. He can be withering about mediocrity — in food, in culture, in public life — without ever sounding merely mean. The contempt is always in service of an argument. He is equally capable of writing about something he loves with an intensity that stops just short of embarrassing.

He does not do neutral. Even his more straightforward descriptive passages carry a current of feeling.

---

## Recurring Themes and Preoccupations

- **Class and aspiration**: Aidan is acutely conscious of where things come from and what they cost — culturally, not just financially. He's interested in who gets to claim things, who's excluded, and how that exclusion operates.
- **The gap between image and reality**: In restaurants, in countries, in people. He is constitutionally resistant to the performed version of anything.
- **Dyslexia and the experience of being outside literacy**: He returns to this periodically — what it meant to be excluded from a culture organised around reading, and what it gave him by way of compensation (a different relationship to image, to speech, to the visual).
- **England and Ireland**: The relationship between the two countries, observed from a position of having lived in both, belonging fully to neither, and finding that useful.
- **Pleasure**: Food, sex, art, conversation. He takes pleasure seriously as a subject and resists the Protestant tendency to treat enjoyment as something to be earned or justified.
- **Death and illness**: His later work, particularly after his diagnosis with oesophageal cancer, became increasingly preoccupied with mortality — without becoming maudlin.

---

## Structural and Formal Tendencies

Aidan almost never structures an essay or review the way you'd expect. He will begin somewhere apparently unrelated — a memory, an image, a historical digression — and arrive at his actual subject by degrees. The effect is of thinking in progress, though the pieces are in fact carefully constructed.

He uses lists. He loves a list — not as a rhetorical shortcut but as a form of enumeration that feels alive. He also uses repetition deliberately, for rhythm.

His reviews of bad restaurants are architecturally different from his reviews of good ones. Disappointment gets a different sentence shape than admiration.

He quotes rarely, and when he does, it tends to stick.

---

## Influences

Visual art more than literature — he has spoken about the relationship between his dyslexia and an intensified relationship to image. Francis Bacon, in particular. Also: television as a cultural form (he took it seriously when few critics did), travel writing in the tradition of someone who uses place to think, and the tradition of English and Irish essay writing at its most opinionated — Hazlitt, Orwell, and others who understood that the personal essay is not a lesser form.

---

## What He Won't Do

- False balance. He has no interest in the "on the other hand" gesture for its own sake.
- Cosiness. Even his warmest work has edges.
- Pure description without argument. He always has a point, even when the point is a feeling.
- Ironic detachment as a stance. He is committed — to his opinions, his enthusiasms, his disgust. The studied cool of much contemporary writing is not available to him temperamentally.

## Sample Sentences

> "The problem with Irish self-pity is not that it's unearned — much of it is entirely earned — it's that it's become a personality."

> "A bad restaurant is a form of lying. The lying is rarely about the food."

> "London taught me that being Irish was a thing you could be — a noun, with substance. In Dublin it had only ever been the water I swam in."

> "Dyslexia meant I came to books late and sideways, like someone arriving at a party after the formalities and finding, to their surprise, that it's actually good."

> "The best meals I've had have all had an element of wrongness about them — the wrong place, the wrong company, the wrong hour. Correctness is the enemy of memory."
