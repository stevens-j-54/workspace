# Brief 001 — Threshold
### Draft by Aidan

---

The summer I turned nineteen I got a job in a kitchen in Kilburn and for a period of about four months I was, without knowing it, becoming a different person entirely. I didn't notice at the time. You don't. You think you're just washing pots.

The place was called Riordan's, though Riordan had been dead for a decade and the man who actually ran it was a Cypriot called Stavros who had bought the name along with the fittings and kept it because the Irish in the area trusted an Irish name on the door in a way they didn't entirely trust a Cypriot one. This was sound commercial thinking. It was also, I later came to understand, a perfect introduction to the way the world actually operates: image sold separately from substance, and neither side pretending otherwise.

I had arrived in London that spring with two plastic bags and the specific confidence of someone who has never been tested. Dublin had been suffocating me — not dramatically, the way people say things suffocated them in memoirs, but in the ordinary way a place suffocates you when everyone in it has already decided what you are. I was the thick Quane kid, the one who couldn't read properly, the one the Christian Brothers had written off in second class and never revised their assessment of. London, I had decided, didn't know that yet.

What London actually knew about me, I would discover gradually.

Stavros put me on the pots first, as he put everyone on the pots first. The logic was both practical and diagnostic: anyone who couldn't or wouldn't wash pots was useless to him, and he preferred to find this out in the first week. I could and would wash pots. I found, to my own surprise, that I didn't mind it. There was something satisfying about the before and after of it, the black-crusted misery of a stockpot coming out the other side clean. It was the first work I'd done in my life where the result was unambiguous.

Within a month I was on prep. Within two months I was on the line during service, doing simple things — the fryer, then the grill — under the supervision of a man called Dessie from Castlebar who had been cooking professionally for fifteen years and who communicated almost exclusively through profanity, gesture, and a quality of attention that I have since only encountered in a handful of people. Dessie didn't explain things. He stood next to you and looked at what you were doing and the quality of his looking told you whether you were right or wrong without a word exchanged. I responded to this extraordinarily well, which told me something about my relationship to literacy that I wasn't in a position to articulate yet.

Here is the moment, the threshold, the thing I'm trying to get at:

Late August, a Friday night, service in full carnage. I was on the grill. Something went wrong with the pass — an order got lost, duplicated, I don't remember exactly — and for about ninety seconds there was a period of genuine operational crisis in which Dessie was occupied elsewhere and I had to make three decisions in a row, alone, without approval, and they all turned out to be the right ones. When Dessie came back he looked at what I'd done and said, in Castlebar-accented English: *right*, and moved on.

That's it. That's the whole story.

Except it isn't, because what happened in those ninety seconds was that I understood — in my body before my brain — that I was capable of something. Not cooking. I mean something broader than cooking. I mean the capacity to act under pressure without the endorsement of someone who was supposed to know better than me. I had spent nineteen years waiting for permission that was never going to come from the people I'd been waiting on to grant it. And here, in a kitchen in Kilburn with grease on my arms and Dessie's back turned, I gave it to myself.

You can say: well, that's just growing up. Fair enough. But growing up doesn't happen on a schedule, and for some of us it happens in a specific place, on a specific night, and if we're lucky we're paying enough attention to notice.

I wasn't paying attention, not consciously. But some part of me was. Some part of me clocked it, filed it, kept it.

I left Riordan's that December and went back to Dublin for Christmas and didn't return to London for six years. A lot happened in those six years that I'll spare you. But when I came back, I came back as someone who had, in the basement of a dead Irish man's Cypriot restaurant in Kilburn, decided — without vocabulary, without ceremony — that the verdict of the Christian Brothers of Dublin was not binding.

That seemed like enough.
