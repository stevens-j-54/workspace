# Brief 001 — Threshold
### Draft by Lucy

---

The year I turned twenty-four I was cleaning houses in Oakland three days a week and the other days I was mostly drinking, though I called it other things at the time. Managing. Unwinding. Having a life.

One of my houses was on Chabot Road, up in the hills. A couple — she was in tech, he was something in law, I never got it quite straight — and two kids who were in school while I worked. The house was the kind of house that makes you think carefully about what you've done with your time. Not enviously, particularly. More like a maths problem you can't stop looking at.

She left me notes. Not instructions, she was careful about that, more like suggestions. *Don't worry about the basement this week*. *If you have time, the windows in the front room could use some attention*. I didn't mind the notes. She was trying to be considerate and mostly she was.

There was a room at the back of the house — the husband's study — where I wasn't really supposed to linger. Not because they'd said so, just because I understood it. I'd go in, straighten things, run the cloth over the surfaces, leave. It took four minutes. There were a lot of books in there, floor to ceiling, and I didn't look at them closely.

Then one week I did.

I don't know why that week specifically. I wasn't in a particular mood about it, I wasn't nursing anything, it was just a Tuesday in November and I pulled a book off the shelf and looked at it. *Their Eyes Were Watching God*. I'd heard of it. I put it back. I pulled out another one. Then another. I stood there for probably fifteen minutes with my cleaning caddy on the floor, reading first pages.

He had good books. Not for show — you can tell when a shelf is for show. These had been read.

I put everything back exactly how I'd found it and went and cleaned the bathroom and didn't think about it. Except I did think about it, the way you think about something when you tell yourself you're not thinking about it.

What I thought was: I could discuss these books. Not all of them. But some of them. Enough of them. I had a good high school English teacher in Albuquerque, the year before we moved to Colorado, and she was one of those teachers who gives you the list and means it. I'd read. I hadn't stopped reading. I read at night when the kids were down, or I meant to, before the wine made it hard to track the sentences.

The thought wasn't: *this is unfair*. I've never found that thought very useful.

The thought was something more uncomfortable than that. It was closer to: *what are you doing with this?*

Not in the room. In general.

I drove home that afternoon on 580 and the sky was the particular grey-orange it gets in November and I thought about the room and the books and Nora Hurston and the fact that I was twenty-four and I had two kids who needed their mother to be a specific kind of present and I was spending too much of my time being absent in a way that had a name I wasn't using yet.

I didn't get sober that day. It was another eleven years before I got sober. I want to be accurate about that so this doesn't become the wrong kind of story.

But something shifted in that room, and I felt it shift. I felt it the way you feel a step that's not where you expected it. A jolt through the whole body, then stillness.

I went back the following Tuesday and cleaned the study in four minutes and didn't look at the shelves. I kept the thought, though. The thought that there was a distance between who I was and who I was going to have to become, and that I was the only one who could cross it.

I kept it until I was ready to do something with it. That took a while. The thought was patient.

Some thoughts are.
