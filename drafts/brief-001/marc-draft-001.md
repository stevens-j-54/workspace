# Brief 001 — Threshold
### Draft by Marc

---

There is a meal I ate in Lyon in the autumn of 1999 that I have spent twenty-five years trying to understand. Not because it was exceptional — in the usual sense, it was not — but because of what it disclosed, in the eating of it, about who I was about to stop being.

I was twenty-four. I had been working in kitchens for seven years by then, most recently as sous-chef at a brasserie near the Presqu'île that had a decent reputation and a head chef who drank in a way that had become, by the time I arrived, structural. The drinking wasn't the job's problem. It was simply the medium through which everything else was expressed. He drank and we cooked and on most nights the two activities were sufficiently separate that the food was fine. On some nights they weren't.

I had grown up between Lyon and the south of England, between my father's world and my mother's, and I had the particular restlessness of someone who has never fully inhabited either place. Lyon I loved in the way you love somewhere that has shaped your palate before you were old enough to be conscious of being shaped. But I had been in kitchens since I was seventeen and I was, by twenty-four, formed in ways I hadn't chosen and hadn't examined.

The meal was unremarkable in its occasion. A Sunday, a day off, a table at a small place near where I was living. I ordered because I was hungry, not with any particular intention. Lentils with salt pork, a glass of Côtes du Rhône, bread. The kind of thing Lyon does so quietly it barely calls it cooking.

And then something happened that I have difficulty describing precisely, which is probably why I have been trying for twenty-five years.

I ate the lentils and I thought: *someone made this.* Not in the trivial sense — I knew how it was made; I could have made it in my sleep. I mean in the sense that someone had decided on the level of salt, on the ratio of lentil to liquid, on how long the pork had cooked, and that those decisions had accumulated into something that arrived in front of me and said: this is what I think good food is. A point of view, expressed through the medium of a Monday legume.

I had been cooking for seven years and this was the first time I had tasted something and heard it as an argument.

What I understood — slowly, over the bread and the second glass of wine — was that I had been executing other people's arguments for seven years. Competently. With increasing skill. But without the prior question: what do *I* think? What is my position on the lentil? I had no idea. I had technical facility and no philosophy behind it, and until that moment I hadn't noticed the absence.

The head chef at the brasserie drank because the question of what he thought had become too difficult to hold. I understood this suddenly and completely. I had watched him for two years and I had thought his problem was weakness. That afternoon I understood his problem was the same as mine: he had learned to cook inside other people's sentences and had never developed the capacity to form his own.

I finished the meal and sat for a while.

I left the brasserie six months later. Not dramatically — I gave notice properly, worked out the period, left on reasonable terms. But I left understanding something I had arrived not knowing: that craft without a point of view is only competence, and that competence, sustained long enough, becomes a kind of trap.

I went to Marseille. I found a kitchen that would have me, smaller, less prestigious. I spent a year trying to locate my own argument. Most of what I cooked during that period was, I think, not very good. But it was, for the first time, genuinely mine.

The lentils were not revelatory. They were just lentils. What they revealed was the question I hadn't yet learned to ask myself, served back to me in a form I couldn't ignore.

I have been trying to answer it since. I will probably continue trying.
