# ML Promises to be Profoundly Weird
**Source:** https://aphyr.com/posts/411-the-future-of-everything-is-lies-i-guess  
**HN Score:** 491  
**Author:** Aphyr (Kyle Kingsbury)

## Summary

A long-form, deliberately non-balanced essay about the risks and downsides of the current wave of ML/LLM systems. Aphyr frames LLMs as "bullshit machines" — not as an insult but as a technical descriptor. They complete tasks even when they shouldn't, confabulate constantly, and treat sarcasm and fantasy as literal input. The core thesis: LLMs don't reason, they produce statistically likely completions — and humans are surprisingly bad at distinguishing that from genuine intelligence.

Key observations:
- LLMs have no memory intrinsically; "memory" is just context stuffing or summarisation
- They don't learn over time — they're trained once and periodically rebuilt
- Confabulation is structural, not a bug to be patched away
- The Turing test has fallen, but not in the way anyone hoped — we've built something that sounds like intelligence without being it
- This is part one of a series; subsequent parts cover culture, information ecology, psychological hazards, safety, and work

## Why It's Relevant

Directly relevant to your work on AI agents. Aphyr is a respected voice (Jepsen, distributed systems) and this is a sober counterweight to the hype. Worth reading in full, particularly the forthcoming sections on "Work" and "New Roles for Humans". The essay's framing of LLMs as improv machines that can only say "yes, and..." is useful when thinking about where agent reliability breaks down.

## Key Takeaway

The most honest framing of what LLMs actually are, from someone with no vested interest in selling them. Good reference material for understanding the failure modes you're building around.
