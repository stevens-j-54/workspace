# Brief 001 — Threshold

**Format:** Short story
**Length:** 1,500–3,000 words (suitable for literary magazine inclusion)
**Status:** Draft — awaiting review

---

## Overview

A short autobiographical piece — or one that draws closely from life — in which the narrator looks back on a specific moment in their early adulthood that served as a threshold: a point at which they crossed, without fully knowing it at the time, from one version of themselves into another.

The story should carry the weight of retrospection. The narrator is older now. They understand something they didn't understand then. The writing should hold that gap — between the person who lived it and the person who is telling it — without overexplaining it.

---

## The Moment

The piece should centre on a single, concrete experience — a conversation, a journey, a failure, a departure, a night that ran too long. Not a montage of formative years, but one scene, or a small cluster of scenes tightly bound together.

The moment itself need not be dramatic. A quiet realisation often lands harder than a crisis. What matters is that something shifted — the narrator's relationship to themselves, to someone they loved, to work, to home, or to what they thought their life was going to be.

---

## Tone

Retrospective but not sentimental. The narrator has some distance from the experience; they can see it clearly enough to write about it, but the emotion should be present underneath the prose, not announced in it. Resist the urge to tell the reader what to feel or to tidy the experience into a lesson.

---

## Voice

First person. The narrator's voice should feel lived-in — specific, unhurried, honest. We should hear a particular person, not a literary exercise.

---

## Fictionalised Elements

The piece may fictionalise names, details, or circumstance. The emotional truth should remain intact. If significant departures from autobiography are made, the author should note this briefly alongside the submission.

---

## What We're Looking For

A story that earns its ending. Not a twist — but a moment where the light falls differently. The reader should finish the piece and sit with it for a moment before moving on.

We are particularly interested in moments that resist easy categorisation: not straightforwardly triumphant, not straightforwardly tragic, but true.

---

## Submission Notes

Authors are encouraged to write from their own experience without overpolishing. A draft that trusts its material is more interesting to us than a refined piece that has lost contact with it.
